The British Commonwealth of Nations

The British Commonwealth of Nations is a political association of 54 member states, almost all of which are former territories of the British Empire. It began to take form in the early 20th century when the self-governing countries within the British Empire came toghether for Imperial Conferences. In 1926 in the Balfour Declaration at the conference, Britain and its dominions agreed they were "equal in status, in no way subordinate one to another in any aspect of their domestic or external affairs, though united by common allegiance to the Crown, and freely associated as members of the British Commonwealth of Nations" .

Statute of Westminister

Later on these aspects to the relationship were formalised by the Statute of Westminister in 1931. The statute increased the sovereignty of the self-governing Dominions of the British Empire from the United Kingdom and it was a crucial step in the development of the Dominions as separate states. All dominions applied without the need for ratification except Australia, New Zealand and Newfoundland. They had to ratify the statute for it take effect. Newfoundland never did, as the government voluntarily ended and fovernance reverted to direct control from London. After the Second World War ended, the British Empire was gradually dismantled. Most of her dominions have become independent countries, whether Commonwealth realms (Canada and Australia) or republics, and members of the Commonwealth. The current Commonwealth of Nations was formally constituted by the London Declaration in 1949, which modernised the community and established the member states as "free and equal" and the head of the Commonwealth is currently Queen Elizabeth II.
